What is "Classical" AI?

What is AI?

  • Simulating human brains or human intelligence (?)
  • Performing tasks by machine (or software) which would ordinarily require human intelligence
  • Making decisions to achieve goals

What is AI? (cont.)

  • Programming machines to learn by themselves (?)
  • Machine Learning (ML) is an important sub-set of AI, but there are many other AI techniques.
  • We'll learn ML in the study block 2.

What is AI? (cont.)

  • Programming machines to possess general intelligence, self-awareness, consciousness (?)
  • Dream too BIG for now - only in sci-fi, maybe would come true one day
  • Programming machines to carry out (or learn to carry out) a specific type of task

What is classical AI?

  • Also know as Good Old Fashioned AI , or Symbolic AI
  • Based on symbolic ("human-readable") representations of problems, logical systems, search spaces
  • As opposed to machine learning, evolutionary algorithms etc. which tend to be black-box next study block

  • 1950: Computer Machinery and Intelligence - Alan Turing proposed a test of machine intelligence (Turing Test).

  • 1956: Artificial Intelligence - the term was coined in Dartmouth workshop by John McCarthy and others.

The Birth of Modern AI!

  • In 1950s, the genius British computer scientist Alan Turing proposed his most important machine intelligence test, which is known as Turing Test today! We will talk about this test in the next slide.
  • In 1952, Arthur Samuel created a programme that could learn to play checkers game independently. That's the first time ever that a computer programme really learnt to do something by its own.
  • In 1956, the Dartmouth workshop organised by Johan Macarthy and other scientists coined the term Artificial Intelligence. That was the first time AI was considered to be an independent area of computer science.

Turing Test

Can C tell if A and B are human or machine
based on the responses to written questions?

  • Let's talk about Turing Test. Has any one heard about this test before? Can you explain to class what the test is about?
  • OK, the turing test is very simple. A computer and a person seat behind a wall. All we need to do during the test is to answer the question if A and B are human beings or machine purely based on their responses to written questions. Apparently, you cannot see them, or wave your hands to or even try to talk to the person - all these sorts of communications are forbidden and you have to rely on written text! A and B may switch seats randomly whenever they want.
  • If the person C cannot tell which one is machine and which one is human, we then consider that the machine passes the Turing Test. In other words, it has the equivalent level of intelligence as a human being!

Modern AI

John McCarthy, Marvin L. Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude E. Shannon (1956)

  • This is a picture of the original Dartmouth workshop proposal. And it was held in the summer of 1956 successfully, announcing the birth of modern AI! It was the origin of modern AI concept.
  • During the Dartmouth workshop, the pioneer scientists discussed some aspects of AI, which are considered as the key requirements of any AI techniques.

Key requirements of AI

  1. Abstractions
  2. Automatic computer
  3. Creativity
  4. Language understanding
  5. Use of neuron nets
  6. Computational efficiency
  7. Self-improvement - machine learning
  • These key requirements include: automatic computer - well, easy to understand as we are talking about intelligence, so the machine needs to compute without being told explicitly. Language understanding - fair enough as we need to interact with machines so they need to understand some kinds of agreed languages with certain rules. Use of neuron nets - biologists learnt from human brains far early than we thought. The basic perceptron model has been proposed at that time. And the computer scientists in Dartmouth workshop realised the importance of neuron nets already. We have to admit their insights on this area. Nowadays, various deep neural networks show their potential in so many ways. Computational efficiency is very important. In theory, a monkey can produce the full set of Shakespeare's manuscripts by typing randomly on the keyboard - it just takes times to get it in the correct order! But for most of our problems, we need the results in a certain period of time

AI Development Stages

3 stages: 1950's-1980's; 1980's-2010's; 2010's-now

Stage I: 1950's - 1980's (automation)

  • 1951: 1st working AI Programme - can play checkers and chess at the University of Manchester.
  • 1957: 1st artificial neural network - designed by Frank Rosenblatt.
  • 1967: Nearest neighbour - a Computer-based pattern recognition algorithm was proposed.
  • 1979: Stanford Cart - the 1st self-driving vehicle by Stanford University students.

Automated-way of simulating human actions.
- by following defined rules!

  • 2006: Deep Learning - coined by Geoffrey Hinton to explain new algorithms that empower computers to distinguish objects and images and video.

The AI Continuum

  • Assistant Intelligence: AI automates basic repetitive and standardised tasks, working from clearly defined rules. Humans are still making the key decisions. - Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)
    Examples: automated assembly line robots, software-based agents.
  • Augmented Intelligence: AI enhances the ability of humans to accomplish tasks - humans and machines learn from each other. - Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
    Examples: virtual assistants, some of generative design systems.
  • Autonomous Intelligence: AI takes over some decision-making, but only after a human fully trusts the machine. - Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI)
    Examples: auto-pilot and self-driven cars.

AI vs. ML vs. DL

AI -> ML -> DL

AI vs. ML vs. DL: Examples

  • Artificial Intelligence: try to play some games with well-defined rules like Chess, Checker, and Jeopardy; encode/decode information.

  • Machine Learning: helps to make decision based on knowledge learnt from big data, e.g. Spam email filters, Face detection, Hand-wirtten digits recognition, etc.

  • Deep Learning: be able to generate contents and be creative with some intelligence, e.g. GO, Poker; ChatGPT, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion; Self-Driving.

AI vs. ML vs. DL: Definitions

  • Artificial Intelligence: a programme that can sense, reason, act, and adapt.

  • Machine Learning: algorithms whose performance improve as they are exposed to more data over time.

  • Deep Learning: subset of machine learning in which multi-layer neural networks learn from vast amounts of data.

The boundaries between these three areas are occasionally ambiguous!
