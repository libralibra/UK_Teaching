The Birth of Modern AI!

  • In 1950s, the genius British computer scientist Alan Turing proposed his most important machine intelligence test, which is known as Turing Test today! We will talk about this test in the next slide.
  • In 1952, Arthur Samuel created a programme that could learn to play checkers game indepndently. That's the first time ever that a computer programme really learnt to do something by its own.
  • In 1956, the Dartmouth workshop organised by Johan Macarthy and other scientists coined the term Artificial Intelligence. That was the first time AI was considered to be an independent area of computer science.

Turing Test

Can C tell if A and B are human or machine
based on the responses to written questions?

  • Let's talk about Turing Test. Has any one heard about this test before? Can you explain to class what the test is about?
  • OK, the turing test is very simple. A computer and a person seat behind a wall. All we need to do during the test is to answer the question if A and B are human beings or machine purely based on their responses to written questions. Apparently, you cannot see them, or wave your hands to or even try to talk to the person - all these sorts of communications are forbidden and you have to rely on written text! A and B may switch seats randomly whenever they want.
  • If the person C cannot tell which one is machine and which one is human, we then consider that the machine passes the Turing Test. In other words, it has the equivalent level of intelligence as a human being!

Advancement of Modern AI

3 stages: 1950's-1980's; 1980's-2010's; 2010's-now

Stage I: 1950's - 1980's (automation)

  • 1951: 1st working AI Programme - can play chckers and chess at the University of Manchester.
  • 1957: 1st artificial neural network - designed by Frank Rosenblatt.
  • 1967: Nearest neighbour - a Computer-based pattern recognition algorithm was proposed.
  • 1979: Stanford Cart - the 1st self-driving vehicle by Stanford University students.

Automated-way of mimicing human actions.
- by following defined rules!

Stage II: 1980's - 2010's (learning)

  • 1981: Explanation Based Learning (EBL) - learnt general problem-solving techniques by observing human solutions.
  • 1985: NETtalk - a program that learns to pronounce words by Terry Sejnowski and Charles Rosenberg.
  • 1989: CNN- for recognising images by Yann LeCun, Yoshua Bengio, and Patrik Haffner.
  • 1997: Deep Blue - IBM's AI programme dereats the world Champion of Chess.
  • 2006: Deep Learning - coined by Geoffrey Hinton to explain new algorithms that empower computers to distinguish objects and images and video.

From knowledge-driven approach to data-driven approach.
- where machine starts learning!

Stage III: 2010's - now

  • 2010: Microsoft Kinect - allows people to interact with computer through movements and gestures.
  • 2011: IBM Watson - AI programme beats two of Jeopardy's greatest champions.
  • 2014: GAN - generative adversarial network developed by Ian Goodfellow.
  • 2016: Google AlphaGo - beat the Korean 9-dan professional Go players Lee Sedol. - A historical match!
  • 2017: Google AlphaGo - beat the world’s number 1 ranking Chinese Go players Ke Jie. - running on a single computer!
  • 2018: GPT model - a prominent framework for generative artificial intelligence by OpenAI.
  • 2021: DALL-E - deep learning model that generates images from text by OpenAI.

The AI Continuum

  • Assistant Intelligence: AI automates basic repetitive and standardised tasks, working from clearly defined rules. Humans are still making the key decisions.
    Examples: automated assembly line robots, software-based agents.
  • Augmented Intelligence: AI enhances the ability of humans to accomplish tasks - humans and machines learn from each other.
    Examples: virtual assistants, some of generative design systems.
  • Autonomous Intelligence: AI takes over some decision-making, but only after a human fully trusts the machine.
    Examples: auto-pilot and self-driven cars.

AI vs. ML vs. DP: Examples

  • Artificial Intelligence: try to play some games with well-defined rules like Chess, Checker, and Jeopardy; encode/decode information.

  • Machine Learning: helps to make decision based on knowledge learnt from big data, e.g. Spam email filters, Face detection, Hand-wirtten digits recognition, etc.

  • Deep Learning: be able to generate contents and be creative with some intelligence, e.g. GO, Poker; ChatGPT, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion; Self-Driving.

AI vs. ML vs. DP: Definitions

  • Artificial Intelligence: a programme that can sense, reason, act, and adapt.

  • Machine Learning: algorithms whose performance improve as they are exposed to more data over time.

  • Deep Learning: subset of machine learning in which multi-layer neural networks learn from vast amounts of data.

The boundaries between these three areas are occasionally ambiguous!
